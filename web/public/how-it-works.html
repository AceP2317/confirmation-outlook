This is a demo of a tool that predicts which orders a factory is about to fail to deliver on time, before it happens, instead of just telling you after the fact what already went wrong.

The problem this solves

Imagine a factory promises a customer: "your order will be ready by this date." Sometimes it can keep that promise, and sometimes it can't: maybe a part is missing, maybe a supplier is late. The share of promises the factory actually keeps in a given week is called the confirmation rate. Northpoint Manufacturing (the fictional company in this demo) aims for 95% and usually lands in the low 90s.

A normal report just tells you last week's number after the fact: a rear-view mirror. The problem: that weekly number bounces around almost randomly from one week to the next, so trying to predict next week's percentage is close to guessing. This tool does something more useful instead: rather than guessing the percentage, it points at the specific parts that are about to cause trouble, early enough to actually do something about it.

Why not just predict next week's percentage?

Because the data doesn't support it. Line up Northpoint's weekly confirmation rate against itself one week later, and the two barely move together at all: statistically close to a coin flip.

So instead of chasing a number that has almost no memory, the tool looks one level down, at individual parts, where the story turns out to be very different (see the next section).

How it actually decides what's risky

In plain terms, it watches two things about every part: has this part already been a problem recently, and is its incoming supply falling behind what's been ordered (call that "running short"). Those two yes/no questions turn every part, every week, into one of four buckets, and history shows those four buckets have very different odds of trouble next week.

Show the actual odds for each bucket

This isn't a black box or an AI guess: it's counting. Out of every part that has ever sat in each bucket, how many actually caused a problem the following week:

SituationChance of trouble next week
Quiet now, supply on track1.9%
Quiet now, but supply running short17.9%
Already a problem, supply on track53.6%
Already a problem and supply running short89.3%

A part running short on supply is roughly 9–11× more likely to cause a fresh problem than a quiet one, even before it's shown any trouble yet: that's the tool's earliest warning sign. A couple of other warning signs work the same way: a part whose demand forecast has been consistently wrong, one running low on safety stock, and one that's near the end of its sales life all raise the odds further on their own.

Show how a risk % becomes a ranked to-do list

A risk percentage alone isn't enough to decide what to work on first: a near-certain problem on 5 units matters less than a 50/50 problem on 2,000 units. So the tool multiplies the two together:

risk % × units currently at stake = expected units at risk

That number is what actually ranks the to-do list. Each flagged part is also sorted into recoverable (there's still a real fix: expedite a shipment, correct a forecast, rebuild stock) or structural (the part is past its official end-of-sale date, so there's no lever left to pull). Right now about 90% of the at-risk volume is still recoverable; the rest is flagged honestly as a lost cause rather than padded into the "fixable" pile.

How we know it's not just making this up

Before trusting any of this, the tool is graded like a pop quiz: the most recent week of data is hidden from it, it's made to guess that week blind using only the week before, and then the guess is checked against what actually happened.

That last number matters: about three-quarters of next week's problems are just this week's problems repeating: anyone could "predict" those. The tool is only given credit for the genuinely new catches, which is why the 11.1× figure is reported separately rather than blended in.

What's the "42-point check" mentioned in the app?

Before this demo is ever allowed to publish a single number, it has to pass a built-in exam: 42 automatic checks that run every time the tool is built. A few examples of what it checks: that more warning signs always mean more risk, never less; that the tool's guesses actually hold up against a week it never saw; and that the prediction code is never secretly peeking at the "answer key" used to generate the practice data. If any check fails, the build itself fails, so a broken tool can't quietly ship.

Worth knowing before you trust any of it

  • Northpoint Manufacturing isn't real. Every order, part, and factory here is generated by a computer program for this demo; no real company's data is used.
  • The weekly percentage itself still can't be predicted well. That's exactly why this tool focuses on individual parts instead of trying to guess next week's number.
  • The total-volume estimate is naturally fuzzy (typically off by about 13%). The ranked list of specific at-risk parts is the trustworthy piece, not the grand total.
  • This is a decision-support demo, not a real planning system. It points at what to check; it doesn't replace one.
