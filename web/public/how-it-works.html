This is a demo of a tool that predicts which orders a factory is about to fail to deliver on time, before it happens, instead of just telling you after the fact what already went wrong.

The problem this solves

Imagine a factory promises a customer: "your order will be ready by this date." Sometimes it can keep that promise, and sometimes it can't: maybe a part is missing, maybe a supplier is late. The share of promises the factory actually keeps in a given week is called the confirmation rate. Northpoint Manufacturing (the fictional company in this demo) aims for 95% and usually lands in the low 90s.

A normal report just tells you last week's number after the fact: a rear-view mirror. The problem: that weekly number bounces around almost randomly from one week to the next, so trying to predict next week's percentage is close to guessing. This tool does something more useful instead: rather than guessing the percentage, it points at the specific parts that are about to cause trouble, early enough to actually do something about it.

Why not just predict next week's percentage?

Because the data doesn't support it. Line up Northpoint's weekly confirmation rate against itself one week later, and the two barely move together at all: statistically close to a coin flip.

So instead of chasing a number that has almost no memory, the tool looks one level down, at individual parts, where the story turns out to be very different (see the next section).

How it actually decides what's risky

In plain terms, it watches two things about every part: has this part already been a problem recently, and is its incoming supply falling behind what's been ordered (call that "running short"). Those two yes/no questions turn every part, every week, into one of four buckets, and history shows those four buckets have very different odds of trouble next week.

Show the actual odds for each bucket

This isn't a black box or an AI guess: it's counting. Out of every part that has ever sat in each bucket, how many actually caused a problem the following week:

SituationChance of trouble next week
Quiet now, supply on track1.9%
Quiet now, but supply running short17.9%
Already a problem, supply on track53.6%
Already a problem and supply running short89.3%

A part running short on supply is roughly 9–11× more likely to cause a fresh problem than a quiet one, even before it's shown any trouble yet: that's the tool's earliest warning sign. A couple of other warning signs work the same way: a part whose demand forecast has been consistently wrong, one running low on safety stock, and one that's near the end of its sales life all raise the odds further on their own.

Show how a risk % becomes a ranked to-do list

A risk percentage alone isn't enough to decide what to work on first: a near-certain problem on 5 units matters less than a 50/50 problem on 2,000 units. So the tool multiplies the two together:

risk % × units currently at stake = expected units at risk

That number is what actually ranks the to-do list. Each flagged part is also sorted into recoverable (there's still a real fix: expedite a shipment, correct a forecast, rebuild stock) or structural (the part is past its official end-of-sale date, so there's no lever left to pull). Right now about 90% of the at-risk volume is still recoverable; the rest is flagged honestly as a lost cause rather than padded into the "fixable" pile.

How we know it's not just making this up

Before trusting any of this, the tool is graded like a pop quiz: the most recent week of data is hidden from it, it's made to guess that week blind using only the week before, and then the guess is checked against what actually happened.

What's the "42-point check" mentioned in the app?

Before this demo is ever allowed to publish a single number, it has to pass a built-in exam: 42 automatic checks that run every time the tool is built. A few examples of what it checks: that more warning signs always mean more risk, never less; that the tool's guesses actually hold up against a week it never saw; and that the prediction code is never secretly peeking at the "answer key" used to generate the practice data. If any check fails, the build itself fails, so a broken tool can't quietly ship.

Every symbol, number, variable, and equation, explained

Everything below is grouped by kind rather than by tab, so it doubles as a lookup table. Symbols are the visual marks on screen; numbers are the fixed thresholds every measurement gets checked against; variables are the named states a part can be in; equations are the actual math, written out in plain words first and then as the literal formula.

Symbols

† next to a part number means that part is past its official end-of-sale date: "structural," no fix left to try.

▲ / ▼ / ▬ (red up, green down, gray flat) next to a category or plant bar show whether this week's number moved more than 5% versus last week, in either direction, or held roughly flat.

+ / − is the open/close mark on every drillable section like this one.

The small circled "?" next to a label on the live dashboard is a hover tooltip: point at it for a one-line plain-English definition, right where you need it.

Solid vs. hatched (striped) bars in the register's "Impact" column: solid amber is a real, measured number (units already unconfirmed today); a hatched, striped bar is an estimate for a part that hasn't failed yet, an early-warning proxy, not a hard count. Drawn differently on purpose so they're never mistaken for the same kind of number.

× after a number (like "10×") always means a ratio, not a percentage: "ten times as often," not "ten percent."

"n=" in a tooltip states the sample size behind a percentage, for example "n=1,234" means that percentage was counted over 1,234 historical examples. More examples behind a number means it can be trusted more.

Darker amber shading on the risk-table heatmap means a higher measured chance of failure; the darkest cells also flip their text to a higher-contrast color once the risk passes 45%, purely so the number stays readable.

☀ / ☽ (sun / moon) toggles the page between light and dark.

Numbers (fixed thresholds and limits)

95% is Northpoint's confirmation-rate target.

42 is the number of automatic checks ("assertions") the tool must pass on every build before it's allowed to publish a single number.

50 is the minimum number of historical examples a detailed, three-signal risk percentage needs before it's trusted; below that, the tool quietly falls back to a simpler, two-signal percentage that has more examples behind it.

60 is the size of the "worklist," the top-ranked group of recoverable parts used to score how often the highest-priority flags are actually right.

15% and 50% are the confidence-tier cut points: Watch is below 15%, Medium is 15% up to 50%, High is 50% and above.

50% forecast accuracy is the line below which a part is flagged "forecast-weak."

One month of stock coverage is the line below which a part is flagged "coverage-thin."

88% to 96% is the trajectory chart's fixed vertical scale, a deliberate zoom-in so week-to-week wobble is visible at all (on a full 0-100% scale it would look like a flat line).

0.35 is the "memoryless" cutoff: below this autocorrelation score, the tool treats the weekly percentage as having no real memory and doesn't try to forecast it directly.

400 is how many register rows show on screen at once, for speed; exporting to Excel is never capped.

5 questions per hour per visitor, plus a small shared daily budget, is the Ask tab's limit on this free public demo. Hitting it shows "rate limited"; a missing API key on this particular deployment shows "offline" (every other tab still works without one).

12 distribution centers (DCs) exist in total; the plant-concentration chart shows the top 6 by unconfirmed volume.

T+1 through T+4: the horizon chart looks exactly one to four weeks ahead, no further.

Variables (named states and terms)

Failing: this part already has unconfirmed units this week.

Receipt-short ("short"): this part's incoming supply doesn't cover what's been ordered.

Stressed: at least one of three warning flags is up for this part (forecast-weak, coverage-thin, or on the end-of-sale list).

Structural: past end-of-sale, so there's no recovery lever left to pull.

Recoverable: not structural, meaning there's still a real fix available.

Emerging: hasn't failed yet, but is showing a warning sign, so it earns a spot on the register ahead of time.

Confidence tier: Watch, Medium, or High, read directly off the measured risk percentage (see the Numbers group above for the cut points).

P(fail) / risk probability: the measured chance a part misses confirmation next week.

Expected units: risk probability multiplied by units currently at stake, the number that actually ranks the to-do list.

Shortfall: a forward-looking estimate of exposure for a part that hasn't failed yet, kept on a deliberately separate scale from expected units so the two are never blended.

Category / cause: which of five reasons a miss is attributed to: Supply (a part is missing or delayed from a supplier), Forecasting (the demand plan was wrong), Logistics (a shipping or transit problem), Customer (the customer changed, held, or blocked the order), or Other (a catch-all, including parts already past end-of-sale). Right now Supply is the biggest driver at roughly 5,900 unconfirmed units, with the other four each in the 1,100 to 1,600 range.

DC: Distribution Center, a warehouse or plant. Right now DC D01 alone carries roughly 4,300 unconfirmed units, more than double the next-largest, a real, common supply-chain pattern where a handful of locations carry most of the risk.

Lever: the suggested fix, one of five fixed options tied to cause (see the Equations group below for the full cascade).

Persistence floor: how often already-failing parts simply fail again.

Leading signal: how much more often a receipt-short (but not-yet-failing) part fails next week, versus a quiet one.

Recall: of all the parts that actually failed, what share the tool had already flagged in advance.

Precision: of the flagged worklist, what share actually did fail.

MAPE: Mean Absolute Percentage Error, the typical size of a forecasting miss, stated as a percent.

Dispersion band: the mean ± one standard deviation range shown on the trajectory chart (see "dispersion vs. confidence interval" in the Equations group).

Autocorrelation: a 0-to-1 score for how much this week's number predicts next week's.

Exposure: units at stake, not dollars.

Supplier: Local or Overseas, whether a part's supplier is nearby or across an ocean.

Equations

Confirmation rate. Confirmed units divided by all ordered units (confirmed plus unconfirmed), as a percent.

rate = 100 × confirmed ÷ (confirmed + unconfirmed)

Week-over-week trend. Up if this week is more than 5% above last week, down if more than 5% below, otherwise flat.

Expected units. Risk probability times how many units are on the line right now.

expected units = risk % × unconfirmed units

Confidence tier. The risk probability sorted into three buckets: Watch below 15%, Medium from 15% up to 50%, High at 50% and above.

Lift. How many times more often flagged parts fail than unflagged ones.

lift = P(fail | flag) ÷ P(fail | no flag)

The lever cascade. Every flagged part gets exactly one fix, chosen in this order: past end-of-sale → "reallocate, no recovery lever"; else receipt-short → "expedite inbound receipt / PO follow-up"; else forecast-weak → "correct the demand plan for this material"; else coverage-thin → "rebuild safety stock / raise coverage"; else → "review order book with the customer team."

Persistence floor. Of parts already failing this week, what share fail again next week, no early-warning skill required.

floor = (failing parts that recur) ÷ (all currently-failing parts)

Net recall. Of every part that actually failed, what share had already been flagged.

recall = (flagged parts that failed) ÷ (all parts that failed)

Worklist precision. Of the top 60 flagged parts by expected units, what share actually failed.

precision = (top 60 that failed) ÷ 60

The volume forecast. Start with a simple estimate: for every currently-failing part, its risk probability times its current unconfirmed units, all added up. Then stretch that number upward, because history shows a predictable share of next week's total trouble comes from parts that haven't failed before at all, not just repeats of today's problems. The stretch factor is one divided by (one minus that historical "brand-new" share).

volume forecast = (simple estimate) ÷ (1 − historical share of brand-new failures)

Dispersion band vs. a confidence interval. The shaded band on the trajectory chart is the average of the last 12 weeks, plus and minus one "typical wobble" (a standard deviation). It describes how much the number has bounced around in the past; it is not a formal statistical guarantee about next week, the way a weather forecaster's "70% chance of rain" is.

band = mean ± 1 standard deviation (12 weeks)

Autocorrelation / "memoryless." A single 0-to-1 score for how much this week's percentage predicts next week's; near 0 means next week is close to a coin flip regardless of this week's result. Northpoint measures 0.06, so the tool treats the headline rate as having no useful memory and predicts individual parts instead.

How the practice data itself was built (hidden from the tool)

Everything above is what the tool computes, live, from data it's allowed to see. The practice data itself was built by a separate, one-time generator that works from its own hidden rulebook, deliberately kept away from the tool being tested. In broad strokes: a hidden table decides how likely each fake part is to fail next week based on its current state; the receipt-short warning sign is "sticky," meaning once a part goes short it's very likely to stay short the next week too, the way real supply problems tend to persist rather than resolve overnight; and two of the twelve fake warehouses are deliberately made more failure-prone than the rest, so the concentration pattern the tool finds (a few locations carrying most of the risk) isn't an accident, it's a pattern planted on purpose for the tool to go and rediscover honestly.

The complete recipe, every statistical parameter used to build the practice world, is written up in full in the technical methodology document.

Worth knowing before you trust any of it

  • Northpoint Manufacturing isn't real. Every order, part, and factory here is generated by a computer program for this demo; no real company's data is used.
  • The weekly percentage itself still can't be predicted well. That's exactly why this tool focuses on individual parts instead of trying to guess next week's number.
  • The total-volume estimate is naturally fuzzy (typically off by about 13%). The ranked list of specific at-risk parts is the trustworthy piece, not the grand total.
  • This is a decision-support demo, not a real planning system. It points at what to check; it doesn't replace one.
